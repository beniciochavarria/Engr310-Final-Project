Unconscious Bias

Unseen Influence: Understanding and Overcoming Unconscious Bias Across Sectors

Benicio Chavarria April 15th, 2025

Letter of Transmittal

To: Linda McMahon, U.S. Secretary of Education

My name is Benicio Chavarria, and I am a student at Washington University in St. Louis, pursuing a double major in Computer Science and Business Administration with a Focus in Business Analytics. I care about truth, transparency, and making sure that people — especially students — have accurate, nuanced information about the systems that affect their lives. It is in this spirit that I submit this final research report on unconscious bias, an issue with profound implications in education and other critical institutions in our society.

This report, “Unseen Influence: Understanding and Overcoming Unconscious Bias Across Sectors,” explores how unconscious bias is formed, how it impacts real-world decisions and behaviors in education, politics, business, and healthcare, and what steps can be taken to mitigate its effects. It is intended for decision-makers, educators, policymakers, and civic leaders who are committed to addressing inequality at its roots.

When I first proposed this project, my focus was narrower: I planned to examine how media consumption affects the physiological and political responses of college students. However, with further research and idealism, I discovered that unconscious bias is not only the deeper, larger problem behind media influence, but also the source of inequities that the gifted, privileged majority faces as a group concerning opportunity, access, and perception. This realization led me to refine my project’s direction—to center it on the psychological and institutional dynamics of unconscious bias and how they must be addressed if we are to create truly inclusive and equitable systems of learning and governance.

What follows is a report driven by this rationale, combining scientific research with sector-specific analysis that we hope will not only provide insight, but also offer actionable recommendations for change. I trust it will play a small part in the continuing struggle to bring fairness, awareness, and accountability to our nation’s educational systems and beyond.

Introduction

Unconscious bias is the automatic, involuntary assumptions people have based on stereotypes, culture, conditioning, and past experience. Whereas your explicit prejudice is domains of your mind that are more conscious and controlled, unconscious bias can happen outside your awareness and can influence how you see, treat, and conduct yourself toward other people and the things around them.

Psychologically, we understand that unconscious bias is just one of these so-called cognitive shortcut heuristics that the brain uses to process information quickly. These heuristics can save time, but they can warp thinking and have a tipping effect that ends up prolonging inequality. Neuroscientific research shows that such biases can be traced to rapid, automatic responses in the brain’s amygdala and are deeply shaped by repeated exposure to societal norms and media portrayals.

Understanding and addressing unconscious bias is especially urgent today because its influence extends far beyond individual attitudes. In education, bias might impact teachers’ discipline of students or evaluation of their abilities. In healthcare, it can impact the quality of care and treatment options available for patients from differing racial or gender identities. In business, bias influences hiring decisions, promotions, and organizational culture. And for politics, it skews who is viewed as a legitimate leader or credible voice. If unexamined, unconscious bias quietly perpetuates institutional inequities and undermines public confidence in institutions that are supposed to serve everyone equitably.

This report argues that, despite its subtle and ingrained nature, unconscious bias has a powerful impact on policy, decision-making, and daily life across multiple sectors. Introducing evidence-based interventions that focus on data-driven hiring, equity education, inclusive curriculum change, and standardized clinical protocols can help minimize the impacts of bias and create more equitable systems. This report explores the sources of unconscious bias, its impact across sectors, and remedial measures to help decision-makers identify and guard against this often invisible but deeply consequential force.
